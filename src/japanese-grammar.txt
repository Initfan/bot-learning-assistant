

1. <b>Sentence Structure (Basic Word Order)</b>
Japanese is Subject-Object-Verb (SOV).

Example:
私はリンゴを食べます。
Watashi wa ringo o tabemasu.
I eat an apple.
next

2. Particles
Particles are tiny words that mark the function of a word in a sentence:

&#8594; は (wa) – topic marker
私は学生です。 – I am a student.

&#8594; が (ga) – subject marker
誰が来ますか？ – Who is coming?

&#8594; を (o) – direct object marker
ご飯を食べます。 – I eat rice.

&#8594; に (ni) / へ (e) – direction/time marker
学校に行きます。 – I go to school.

&#8594; で (de) – location of action
図書館で勉強します。 – I study at the library.

&#8594; と (to) – "and" / "with"
友達と話します。 – I talk with my friend.
next

3. Polite vs. Casual Speech

&#8594; Polite: です / ます form
行きます (ikimasu) – I go

&#8594; Casual: dictionary/plain form
行く (iku) – I go
next

4. Verb Tenses

&#8594; Present/future (ます): 食べます – eat/will eat
&#8594; Past (ました): 食べました – ate
&#8594; Negative (ません): 食べません – do not eat
&#8594; Past negative (ませんでした): 食べませんでした – did not eat
next

5. Adjectives
Japanese adjectives come in two types:

&#8594; い-adjectives: 大きい (big)
Present: 大きいです 
Past: 大きかったです

&#8594; な-adjectives: 静か (quiet)
Present: 静かです 
Past: 静かでした
next

6. Question Sentences
Add か to make a polite question:

あなたは学生ですか？ – Are you a student?

In casual speech, rising intonation works:

学生？ – (You're a student?)
next

7. Using "の" for Possession
AのB = B of A / A’s B
私の本 – my book
日本の車 – Japanese car
