

1. Basic Sentence Structure
Chinese is typically Subject–Verb–Object (SVO).

我爱你。
Wǒ ài nǐ.
I love you.
next

2. Pronouns
我。
Wǒ.
I / me.

你。
Nǐ.
You.

他 / 她 / 它。
Tā.
He / she / it.

我们。
Wǒmen.
We.

你们。
Nǐmen.
You (plural).

他们 / 她们。
Tāmen.
They.
next

 3. Negation
&#8594; 不 (bù) – not (for present/future/general)
我不是老师。 – I’m not a teacher.

&#8594; 没 (méi) – not (for past/verb 有)
我没去学校。 – I didn’t go to school.
我没有钱。 – I don’t have money.
next

4. Questions
Yes/No: Add 吗 (ma) at the end
你是学生吗？ – Are you a student?

"What/Where/Who/When/Why/How":

&#8594; 什么 (shénme) – what

&#8594; 哪里 / 哪儿 (nǎlǐ / nǎr) – where

&#8594; 谁 (shéi) – who

&#8594; 什么时候 (shénme shíhou) – when

&#8594; 为什么 (wèishénme) – why

&#8594; 怎么 (zěnme) – how
next

5. Measure Words
Chinese nouns usually require 量词 (liàngcí) = "measure words":

&#8594; 一个人 (yí ge rén) – a person

&#8594; 两本书 (liǎng běn shū) – two books

&#8594; Common measure word: 个 (ge) – general-purpose.
next

6. Word Order: Time and Place
Typical structure:

[Subject] + [Time] + [Place] + [Verb] + [Object]

Example:

我今天在家吃饭。
Wǒ jīntiān zàijiā chīfàn.
I am eating at home today.
next

7. Aspect Markers
Chinese doesn’t change verb forms, but uses particles to indicate aspect:

&#8594; 了 (le) – completed action
我吃了饭。 – I ate (already).

&#8594; 过 (guò) – experience
我去过中国。 – I’ve been to China.

&#8594; 着 (zhe) – ongoing state
他穿着衣服。 – He is wearing clothes.
