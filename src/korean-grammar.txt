

1. Basic Sentence Structure: Subject–Object–Verb (SOV)
Korean sentences usually follow:

Subject + Object + Verb

Example:

나는 사과를 먹어요.
naneun sagwareul meogeoyo.
→ I eat an apple.
("I + apple + eat")
next

2. Particles (조사)
Particles indicate the grammatical function of words. Most common ones:

&#8594; 나는 학생이에요.
Naneun haksaeng-ieyo.
I’m a student.
(은/는 – topic marker)

&#8594; 친구가 왔어요.
Chinguga wasseoyo.
(Friend came.)
(이/가 – subject marker)

&#8594; 사과를 먹어요.
Sagwareul meogeoyo.
(Eat an apple.)
(을/를 – object marker)

&#8594; 학교에 가요.
Hakgyoe gayo.
(Go to school.)
(에 – time/place marker)

&#8594; 집에서 공부해요.
Jibeseo gongbuhaeyo.
(Study at home.)
(에서 – action location marker)

&#8594; 친구랑 가요.
Chingurang gayo.
(Go with a friend.)
(랑 – “and” / “with” particle)
next

3. Verb Endings
No verb conjugation by subject. Instead, conjugation reflects formality and tense.

Tense	Informal Polite	Formal
Present	먹어요 (eat)	먹습니다
Past	먹었어요 (ate)	먹었습니다
Future	먹을 거예요 (will eat)	먹겠습니다

Negative:

안 먹어요 = don’t eat

못 먹어요 = can’t eat
next

4. Adjectives = Verbs
In Korean, adjectives act like verbs and conjugate:

작다 (to be small) &#8594; 작아요

예쁘다 (to be pretty) &#8594; 예뻐요

비싸다 (to be expensive) &#8594; 비싸요
next

5. Honorifics and Politeness
Korean grammar changes based on respect:

&#8594; 요 ending = polite

&#8594; 습니다 = formal polite

반말 (informal) used with close friends, younger people
next

6. Questions
Add a rising tone or 요 ending:

어디에 가요? – Where are you going?

뭐 먹어요? – What are you eating?

7. Conjugation Patterns
고 – and (connects verbs): 먹고 마셔요

지만 – but: 좋지만 비싸요

니까 – because: 바쁘니까 못 가요
